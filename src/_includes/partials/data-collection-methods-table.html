Method Description
Structure Data collection method may be highly structured, indicating what information is to be collected and how, whilst the alternative may be to have a minimum of structure to enable information to be revealed from the participants personal experiences.
Quantifiable If statistics (quantitative research) are to be used, the data must be gathered in a way that enables this to occur, whilst data, which is collected from narrative (qualitative research), is typically collected in word form. As a rule a structured approach to data collection usually yields data which is easier to quantify, though data collected from unstructured tools may also be quantified.
Researcher obtrusiveness The participants may be aware they are taking part in a study or they may be unaware. In the former case this may affect their performance (the Hawthorne effect), whilst in the latter there are a number of ethical problems which may arise.
Objectivity Scientific work generally strives to be as objective as possible, though in some types of research (e.g. phenomenology) the subjective judgement of the researcher is a valuable component in the data collection process. If these are seen as different aspects of data collection, it may well be that the approach taken by a researcher demands one or other characteristic to be dominant.
